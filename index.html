Case 2 — Investor Meeting

The question that was asked three times.

Discovery call. 4 participants. Pre-investment evaluation.

What happened

The evaluator asked about revenue model three times — each time with different wording.

Why it matters: When someone asks the same question three times, your previous answers didn’t land.

What else Darcy caught

Founder opened with “Not really” about traction — then immediately listed examples of traction.

Why it matters: The first words set the anchor. The correction came too late to overwrite it.

The meeting went well on the surface. Both sides were polite. But the evaluator left with two unanswered concerns — neither of which was ever stated directly.

Darcy distinguishes genuine alignment from polite agreement.
Case 3 — Partnership Discussion

Both sides said “let’s move forward.” They meant different things.

6 participants. ~45 minutes. Potential integration between two companies.

What both sides said

“Testing is the next step.”

What each side meant

One side: ready to start tomorrow.
Other side: needs legal review, internal approval, 6–8 weeks minimum.

Neither stated their timeline.

Same word, different meaning

Both sides used “fraud detection” five times in the meeting.

What Darcy found

One side meant behavioral patterns. The other meant document verification.

They never defined the term. They assumed agreement.

The meeting ended with a handshake. The partnership stalled three weeks later when the misalignment surfaced.

Darcy flags assumed agreement — where both sides think they’re aligned but aren’t.
