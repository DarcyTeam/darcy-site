Enterprise Discovery Call · 6 Participants · 45 Minutes

Everyone said yes. But they meant four different things.

A partnership discovery call between two companies. Six people on the call. All polite. All engaged. Standard meeting notes would say “great alignment, next steps pending.” Darcy found four critical gaps hiding in plain sight.

Translation Table
What was said What Darcy detected What it actually meant
“We’ve been thinking about two different tracks — conversational AI onboarding and voice-assisted verification.” These are future directions, not current capabilities. This is a roadmap conversation, not an integration conversation. We’re exploring where you might fit into products we haven’t built yet. This is early-stage, not a procurement decision.
“We’ll have to go through a little bit of red tape. It’s not instant because they’re a big customer.” Data access requires multiple internal approvals. Timeline is weeks to months, not days. This is a real constraint, not a stall. We want to test this but the path to getting real data is genuinely complicated by enterprise customer relationships and PII requirements.
“What would work for us best is if we can get access to the API and see what else we can extract.” This is a hands-on evaluation request. The technical team wants to experiment independently, not receive demos. We’re serious enough to invest engineering time. Give us the tool and let us find the value ourselves.
“To be honest, I don’t think adding voice to that is going to help. The majority of the bottleneck still lies on fraudulent documents.” Honest pushback. This participant is filtering for practical value, not being polite. Their primary pain point is document fraud, not behavioral analysis in real-time. Don’t oversell. Show me where this works for what we actually deal with today, not a hypothetical future product.
What Darcy Caught That Notes Missed
Appeared: Strong Alignment on Voice Integration

Appeared: Ready to Start Testing

Darcy: Four specific gaps. Two misread signals. One honest objection that was the most important buying signal in the room — because when they kept asking about limitations, they were already modelling the implementation.
The Difference

DarcyCode = conversation map + resolution manual.

Sentiment analysis = mood thermometer. It looks at words, tone, maybe emojis and says: “This message is positive / negative / neutral,” or “Speaker sounds angry / happy / sad.” It does not understand what people were trying to achieve, where they misunderstood each other, or how to fix it.

DarcyCode looks at the whole interaction and asks:

What did each person actually need from this conversation?
What did they actually hear versus what was meant?
At which exact moments did “said ≠ heard,” and what pattern caused that?

Then DarcyCode goes one step further and answers “what to do now”:

“When they ask the same question 3 times, your answer isn’t landing. Next time, give one concrete example instead of repeating the abstract explanation.”

“You and the investor both say ‘traction,’ but you mean user stories and they mean ARR. Name the metric explicitly next time.”

“This co-founder resolves conflict fast when you call, not text. After tension, call within 20 minutes and start with one warm line.”

Sentiment analysis

“The call turned negative halfway through; the investor sounded skeptical.”

DarcyCode

“The call turned after you said ‘Not really’ about traction. They heard ‘no traction’ although you later gave examples. Next time, lead with the examples and only then qualify — that prevents the skepticism.”

Sentiment analysis tells you how the room feels. DarcyCode tells you where the communication broke, why it broke, and exactly how to resolve or prevent it.
